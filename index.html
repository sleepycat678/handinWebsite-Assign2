Responsive Redesign: The Rhode Island Historical Society

Overview

In the Responsive Redesign project, our goal is to analyze and identify flaws in an existing interface, redesign the website in order to improve user experience.

I chose to redesign The Rhode Island Historical Society website.

Founded in 1822, the RIHS is the fourth-oldest state historical society in the United States. The RIHS holds the largest and most important historical collections relating to Rhode Island and offers a variety of educational programs, including workshops, lectures, films, and walking tours of Providence.

Part 1: Identifying Usability Problems

Finding Problems
  • Lack of Consistency

    • Currently, there are too many different fonts. Besides, the font sizes and the line spacing are not consistent. We need to keep the number of fonts used at a minimum and make the font sizes and spacing between lines consistent through the entire page.

  • Redundancy

    • Although repetition can bring more attention but sometimes also make it look redundant. For example: Donate buttons are put in 3 different places in the single page. Personally speaking, it can be placed at the top menu with a conspicuous color, that would be enough to draw people's attention.

  • Content Organization

    • The content are not aligned will and the image at the top seems kind of out of the place.

Accessibility

By using WebAIM WAVE, we are able to detect possible accessibility problems. From the results, I think the developers have made some efforts for the accessibility, however, there are still something that can be further improved.

  • Most of the images don't have alternative text

    • It's better to add alt text to the image in case the image fails to load on a user's screen. This text can also help screen-reading tools describe images to visually impaired readers.

  • Contrast errors

    • Low contrast text is bad for usability. Insufficient contrast between the text color and the background degrades the user experience.

  • Redundant link

    • Redundant hyperlinks are a common accessibility issue. Since redundant links are frustrating for users, the best practice is to fix it by combining both elements in the same hyperlink.
